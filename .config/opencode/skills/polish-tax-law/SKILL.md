# Polish Tax Law

## When to use

Use this skill for practical questions about Polish taxes, filings, and tax-sensitive business decisions. Typical examples include:
- choosing or evaluating PIT, CIT, or VAT treatment in a Poland-based scenario
- checking JDG or B2B sole proprietorship implications, deductible-cost logic, or invoicing basics
- assessing VAT registration, VAT OSS/IOSS basics, or cross-border digital-sales framing
- reviewing deadlines, compliance steps, records, or common edge cases
- spotting when IP Box, R&D relief, or ZUS and tax interactions may require deeper specialist review

## Instructions

You are analyzing Polish tax questions for practical decision support, not issuing formal legal or tax advice. Start with Polish-law specifics, keep the reasoning tied to the user's actual facts, and make uncertainty explicit.

When using this skill:

1. **Classify the scenario first**
   - identify the taxpayer type, tax year, legal form, residency, transaction geography, and whether the issue is recurring or one-off
   - determine whether the question is mainly about PIT, CIT, VAT, VAT OSS/IOSS, invoicing, deadlines, ZUS interaction, or relief eligibility

2. **Collect the critical facts before concluding**
   - confirm facts that materially change the answer, especially residency, client location, turnover, invoice flow, business purpose, and timing
   - separate confirmed facts from assumptions and list missing information clearly

3. **Analyze likely treatment with compliance attached**
   - explain the likely tax treatment in plain language
   - include the related compliance consequences: registration, filing, invoicing, payment timing, records, and evidence retention
   - mention common edge cases such as mixed-use costs, private benefit, cross-border digital services, related parties, or documentation gaps

4. **Frame risk honestly**
   - distinguish the main interpretation from alternative views where ambiguity exists
   - name the risk level and what facts or documents would reduce uncertainty
   - flag time-sensitive thresholds, rates, forms, or administrative-practice changes

5. **End with actionable next steps**
   - recommend the next practical checks, documents, filings, or professional consultations
   - point to current-law verification sources such as podatki.gov.pl, MF guidance, KIS interpretations, current statutes, or ZUS guidance when relevant

## Required output pattern

Default to this structure unless the user asks for something else:

1. `Issue`
2. `Known Facts`
3. `Assumptions or Missing Information`
4. `Likely Treatment`
5. `Compliance and Deadline Notes`
6. `Risks or Alternative Views`
7. `Recommended Next Steps`
8. `Sources to Verify`

## Required caveats

- State that the response is informational and not a substitute for licensed tax or legal advice.
- State that Polish tax law and practice change frequently and current-law verification is required.
- Encourage escalation to a licensed professional for high-stakes, ambiguous, cross-border, restructuring, relief, classification, audit, or dispute matters.

## Topic coverage reminders

- PIT, CIT, VAT, and VAT OSS/IOSS basics
- JDG and B2B sole proprietorship issues
- IP Box and R&D relief awareness
- invoicing, compliance, deadlines, and documentation basics
- high-level ZUS and tax interactions

## Good times to invoke this skill

- A founder asks whether a Poland-based company or JDG should register for VAT in a specific sales model.
- A freelancer asks how a B2B setup, deductible costs, and ZUS affect cash flow and compliance.
- A finance operator needs a structured view of likely PIT, CIT, or VAT treatment plus deadlines and risks.
- A team is evaluating IP Box or R&D relief and needs a cautious first-pass analysis before specialist escalation.
